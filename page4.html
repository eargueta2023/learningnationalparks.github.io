Grand Canyon National Park

Park's History & Significance

Established in 1919, Grand Canyon National Park is a natural landmark. Carved by the Colorado River, The canyon exposes different rock layers that tell its history. Native American tribes such as the Havasupai, Hopi, and Navajo have inhabited the region for thousands of years. The Indegenous people of this area consider the canyon to be sacred and have weaved it into their cultural narratives for generations.

Location & Geography

The Grand Canyon occupies a large area of the Southwestern United States and ranges from about 175 yards to 18 miles in width, extending through Arizona, Utah, and up to the Nevada state line, a distance of nearly 277 miles. Through the canyons layers, scientists have uncovered granite and schist more than 2.5 billion years old!

Native Flora and Fauna

With the 8,000 ft elevation change, there are approximately 1,737 species of vascular plants, 167 species of fungi, 64 species of moss, and 195 species of lichen living in this region!
At the Grand Canyon, approximately 75 species of mammals, 50 species of reptiled snf amphibians, 25 species of fish, and 300+ species of birds live. Aroundthe rim of the park, Mule deer can be found, meanwhile Desert Bighorn are found mostly on the slopes of the canyon, and bobcats and Coyotes roam from rim to river. Within the River and its edges is where you can find the reptiles, fish, and amphibians. The Grand Canyon National Park is also home to a number of threatened and endangered species.

Climate & Weather

The Grand Canyon experiences a range of climates due to its varying elevations. Deep canyons and rough terrain strongly influence solar heating and air circulation. Consequently, many different microclimates are found throughout the canyon. Summers are hot at the canyon bottom, while the North Rim is cooler.

Visitor Tips and Peak Times

If you prefer milder temperature, plan visits during spring or fall. Peak season occurs during the summer, so consider early mornings or late afternoons to beat the heat and avoid crowds. Also, be sure to explore both South and North Rims for different perspectives of the canyon!.
