History of National Parks

National Parks are protected areas of significant natural, cultural, and recreational value that are designated and managed by the government. These parks aim to preserve and protect unique ecosystems, landscapes, wildlife, and historical/cultural resources. National Parks also typically offer opportunities for outdoors recreation, education, and research. Their preservation provides enjoyment for all generations!

But what makes a National Park?

Requirements for what makes a park a National park, varies based on a country and its regulations. For the most part, In the United States the requirements are as follows:

Requirements Description
National Significance - It is an outstaning example of a particular resource
- It possesses exceptional value or quality in illusterating or interpreting the natural or cultural themes of our Nation's heritage
- It offers superlative opportunities for recreation for public use and enjoyment, or for scientific study
- It retains a high degree of integrity as a true, accurate, and relatively unspoiled example of the resource.
Resource Evaluation: Natural Areas Natural area examples may include but are not limited to the following:

- A critical refuge that is necessary for the continued survival of a species
- A site that contains rare or unusually abundant fossil deposits
- A site that posesses exceptional diversity of ecological components such as species, communities, or habitats
- A site that posesses exceptional diversity of geological features such as landforms or observational manifestations of geologic processes
Resource Evaluation: Cultural Areas Cultural areas may be historic:

- Districts, sites, building structures
- Objects that possess exceptional value or quality in illustrating or interpreting our heritage
- Objects that possess a high degree of integrity of location, design, setting, materials, workmanship, feeing, and association
Suitability An area must represent a natural or cultural theme or type of a recreational resource that:

- Is not already adequately represented in the National Park System
- Is not comparably represented or protected for public enjoyment by another land-managing entity
Feasibility An area's natural systems and/or historic setting must:

- Be of sufficient size and have appropriate configuration to enure long term protection of the resources and to accommodate public use
- Have potential for efficient administration at a reasonable cost

- Important Feasibility factors include:

                a. Land ownership
                b. Acquisition costs
                c. Life cycle maintenance costs
                d. Access
                e. Threats
                f. Staff/development requirements

History of National Parks

Conservation, preservation, and the recognition of the value of natural and cultural landscapes makes up the history of National parks. The concept of setting aside certain areas for protection and public enjoyment has evolved over time!

Here are some of the key milestones in the history of National Parks

Yellowstone National Park Act, 1872
The Act signed into law on March 1, 1872, established the world's first true national park.

Source: "Yellowstone: First National Park." September 2, 1920. Essex County Herald (Guildhall, VT), Image 2. Chronicling America: Historic American Newspapers.


Act to Establish the National Park Service, 1916 (Organic Act)
Congress passed what is often known as the Organic Act, which established the National Park Service and placed all the existing parks under its management. The legislation established the basis for the fundamental mission, philosophy, and policies of the National Park Service.

Source: "Organic Act", 1916. Library of Congress.


Reorganization of 1933
With the 1933 reorganization and new responsibilities for historical areas, historic preservation became a primary mission of the National Park Service.


Wild and Scenic Rivers Act, 1968
The Wild and Scenic Rivers Act provided for the protection and preservation in free-flowing condition of selected rivers that possessed outstanding scenic, recreational, geologic, fish and wildlife, historic, or cultural values.

Source: "Cache le Poudre Wild and Scenic River." Arapaho and Roosevelt National Forests, Colorado. Photo by Tim Palmer.


National Environmental Policy Act of 1969
The National Environmental Policy Act formed the nation's basic charter for environmental protection

Source: "President Nixon signs the National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA)", January 1, 1970. Accessed via "Legal-Planet.org"


Endangered Species Act of 1973
The Endangered Species Act required federal agencies to ensure that their activities (authorized, funded, or executed) do not jeopardize the existence of any endangered or threatened species of plant or animal (including fish) or result in the destruction or deterioration of critical habitat of such species.

Alaska National Interest Lands Conservation Act, 1980
The Alaska National Interest Lands Conservation Act added more than 47 million acres to the National Park System.

Source: "The National Parks and Monuments in Alaska." Accessed via "nps.gov"


Native American Graves Protection and Repatriation Act, 1990
Earlier legislation had expressly forbidden the excavation of Indian graves and removal of human and ceremonial remains.
